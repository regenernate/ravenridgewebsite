What is the Endocannabinoid System?  

  • Large, cell membrane receptor system comprised of CB1 and CB2 receptors
  • CB1 receptors predominate in the CNS, but are also widely scattered in the peripheral nervous system, and other tissues.  
  • CB2 receptors are found predominantly on muscle, adipose tissue, and immune cells, but are also scattered through the brain, mostly on glial cells.
  • Discovered in 1992 by Dr. Mechoulam and others.

<Image 1>

  • In general the receptor system:
  • Increases energy consumption and storage in response to physical exertion
  • Modulates emotional responses (balances mood)
  • Blunts inflammatory responses
  • Modulates pain
  • Restores homeostasis:
  • Following psychological stress by blunting the effects of inhibitory or excitatory nuerotransmitters in the nervous system
  • Following physical exertion/exercise, by increasing energy storage
  • Following physical injury by modulating pain and inflammatory responses

What endogenous molecules (Endocannabinoids) activate the Endocannabinoid System?

  • The primary endocannabinoids are N-arachidonoylethanolamide (Anandamide:  AEA) and 2-arachidonoylglcyerol (2-AG).
  • Anandamide is manufactured by nerve, muscle, immune and adipose cells.  In the CNS, its actions are primarily mediated through the CB1 receptor, and peripherally primarily through the CB2 receptor.
  • 2-AG is concentrated mainly in the brain where most of its actions are mediated through the CB1 receptor.
  • The endocannabinoids are unique neurotransmitters, in that:
  • they are fatty acid based
  • they are not stored and released by vesicles, but are produced on demand by post-synaptic cells
  • their production and release are primarily stimulated by calcium influx when a post-synaptic cell is stimulated
  • they travel retrograde and attach to CB1 and CB2 receptors on pre-synaptic cells, which decreases calcium influx, thereby modulating release of excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters from the presynaptic cells.
  • In immune/inflammatory response, they modulate T- and B-lymphocyte proliferation, migration, apoptosis, and cytokine production.
  • In muscle cells they reduce energy expenditure
  • In liver and adipose cells they increase glucose uptake and lipogenesis, decrease insulin sensitivity, and decrease lipolysis, resulting in energy storage
  • In addition to activating CB1 and CB2 receptors, endocannabinoids interact with other receptors:
  • TRPV1 (Transient Receptor Potential Vanilloid Type- 1) activation-modulates pain and inflammation
  • PPARG (Peroxisome Proliferator Activated Receptor gamma) activation-resulting in neuroprotection, improved memory and cognition, analgesia, and anti-tumor effects

How does Cannabidiol (CBD) interact with the Endocannabinoid System and other receptors?

  • CBD is a non-psychoactive Phytocannabinoid that interacts with the Endocannabinoid System, some enzymes, and ion channels

NOTE:  WHETHER THESE INTERACTIONS RESULT IN CLINICALLY SIGNIFICANT EFFECTS HAS ONLY BEEN DEMONSTRATED IN HIGH QUALITY CLINICAL TRIALS OF ITS USE IN TREATING SOME SEIZURE DISORDERS

  • CBD is not a ligand for CB1 or CB2 receptors: Its a negative allosteric modulator, binding to the CB1 and CB2 receptors and changing the 3 dimensional shape of their receptor site, modulating the effect of ligands. 
  • It is an antagonist of GPR55 (G Protein-coupled Receptor 55, felt to possibly be an endocannabinoid receptor).  GPR55 inactivation inhibits some cancer cells that over express GPR55 (common in colorectal, pancreatic, and colon cancer).
  • It binds with receptors which are not part of the endocannabinoid system
  • It selectively inhibits COX-2.  COX-2 inhibition results in pain modulation; has an anti-inflammatory effect and anti-tumor effects
  • It activates PPARgamma.  Activation is neuroprotective; improves memory and cognition; has analgesic and anti-tumor effects.
  • It activates TRPV1.  Activation modulates pain and inflammation.
  • It activates the serotonin receptor 5-HT1A (5-Hydroxytryptamine Receptor Subtype 1A).  Activation of 5-HT1A results in an anti-inflammatory, anti-anxiety, anti-depressant effect.
  • It activates the Adenosine A2A receptor, and inhibits Adenosine uptake via Phospholipase-A2.  Activation of A2A and reuptake inhibition has an anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, effect.
  • It deactivates FAAH (Fatty Acid Amide Hydrolase), the enzyme which metabolizes Anandamide and 2-AG, enhancing their effects.  FAAH deactivation results in higher levels of Anandamide in the brain, which has a calming effect, blunting the persistence of the fight-flight response (part of the mechanism for developing PTSD).
  • It has an inhibitory effect on some Na+ and Ca++ ion channels, thus slowing neuron firing.

Where is (CBD) produced in the hemp plant?

  • Its primarily produced In trichomes (sac like structures that are part of the flower)
  • Like all the cannabinoids, its produced as an acid.  In the case of CBD, as CBDA (cannabidiolic acid)
  • CBDA is converted to CBD with light exposure, heat, and aging
  • CBD is the most studied form, but there is emerging evidence that CBDA is a much more potent anti-inflammatory, and possibly anti-cancer molecule.

What is the difference between pure CBD; CBD included in a Full Spectrum Extract; and the entourage effect?

  • Pure CBD has a narrow, bell shaped therapeutic index, requiring high doses to get an effect, with the effect peaking and tapering off quickly with higher doses.  Typical doses are in the 5-20 mg/kg/day for approved indications (up to 50 mg/kg have been used for seizure control).
  • Full spectrum extract containing CBD and the full range of biologically active compounds found in the trichomes requires a much lower dose (typically in the range of 0.5 to 1 mg/kg/day) for the same effect, with a much wider therapeutic window.
  • This is referred to as the entourage effect.  
  • There is a growing body of evidence that this is clinically relevant
  • The evolving field of synergistic network pharmacology, moving past the single molecule, single target model of drug discovery, is ideally suited to further explore this effect

Is it Legal to use CBD products?

Yes.  

The 2018 Farm Bill made it legal to grow hemp.  

The 2020 Farm Bill amended the 2018 Bill, making it legal to sell CBD products made from hemp .  

Has the FDA approved pure CBD for use in any medicines?

  • Yes.  It has approved Epidiolex (Cannabidiol), manufactured by GW Pharmaceuticals, for certains seizure disorders in children: Lennox-Gastaut syndrome; Dravet syndrome; Tuberous Sclerosis Complex.  The mechanism of action is uncertain, but felt to be related to:
  • Inhibition of Adenosine uptake
  • Antagonism of GPR55
  • Activation of TRPV1
  • Inhibitory effects on Na+ and low voltage Ca++ channels

Can CBD be applied on the skin?

  • Yes, but CBD is not water soluble and cannot penetrate into the dermis and subcutaneous tissues.  
  • If the intended effect is related to deeper tissues, an absorption enhancer such as Argan Oil, needs to be added to topical formulation.
  • Argan Oil contains 40-50% Oleic Acid, and 30-40% Linoleic Acid, both of which disrupt the skin barrier and allow deeper penetration

How is CBD metabolized?

  • Major route of metabolism is the liver
  • Primarily by Cytochrome P450 (CYP2C group and CYP3A group). Of note, coumadin is metabolized by a CYP2C enzyme.
  • CBD undergoes significant first pass metabolism in the liver.
  • 90% of orally ingested CBD undergoes first pass metabolism in the liver
  • Because of first pass metabolism, Sublingual/buccal mucosa administration is the preferred route of administration if systemic effects are desired, or topically if local effects are.
  • It can also be smoked and absorbed in the lungs, but long term harmful effects of this route of administration are not known.

Does CBD interact with other medications?

  • There are potential interactions between prescription doses of CBD (pure CBD taken in much higher doses than contained in products made from Full Spectrum Extract) and drugs that are metabolized by CYP1A2; CYP2B6; CYP2C9; CYP2C19; and CYP3A4.  
  • There have been few reports of significant interactions between prescription drugs and sublingual or topical doses of CBD contained in products made from Full Spectrum Extract.
  • The main interactions to watch for would be CBD with drugs with a narrow therapeutic index (NTI) metabolized by the same enzymes.  Penn State pharmacy recently published a list of 57 medications to monitor.  Commonly prescribed drugs on the list include:
  • Warfarin
  • Levothroxin

What Precautions are there?

  • The following is based on experience with Sativex <link to FAQ “Has the FDA approved any medicine made with CBD included in a Full Spectrum Extract?”> in Canada and Europe.  See the reference below for full information.
  • Use in patients with moderate to severe hepatic impairment is not advised.  If used in patients with mild hepatic impairment, LFTs should be monitored to confirm no adverse effect on the liver.  CBD related effects are reversible when CBD use is discontinued.
  • Use in patients with severe cardiovascular disease is not advised
  • Patients with impaired renal function should be monitored for side effects
  • Do not use in women who are pregnant or breast feeding
  • Many of the precautions, like those related to a personal, or family history of psychiatric illness, are related to the THC contained in Sativex.  There is emerging evidence that CBD has a positive or neutral effect on these conditions.
  • Products made from Full Spectrum Extract can contain small amounts of THC which can be detected in high sensitivity drug screening.  If patients are subject to drug testing, they should be made aware of this prior to taking these products

What evidence is there that CBD is effective in treating defined medical conditions?

  • Researchers have identified a large number of receptors, enzymes, and membrane transporters that CBD interacts with, and the effects of those interactions, in human and animal models.  This is an essential first step, but not sufficient to demonstrate clinical efficacy.
  • With the potential mechanisms of action identified, clinical uses of CBD is a hot research topic currently, with some well designed clinical trials currently being conducted in the US and other countries, focused on its use in a wide range of conditions.
  • Most clinical trials reported to date have small numbers of participants, and with few exceptions are not randomized, controlled, blinded trials, which is the standard for proving efficacy.
  • Evidence to date is that CBD is safe in doses used in the reported trials.
